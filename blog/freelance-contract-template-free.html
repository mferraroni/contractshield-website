You've just landed a new client. The brief looks solid, the rate is fair, and you're ready to start. But then you remember: you have no contract.

Most freelancers know they need one. Far fewer actually have one that works. This guide covers the 7 best free freelance contract templates available in 2025 — what each one covers, where it falls short, and which one is worth actually using.

What a Freelance Contract Actually Needs to Cover

Before comparing templates, know what you're looking for. A contract that protects you must cover:

  • Scope of work — exactly what you'll deliver, in writing
  • Payment terms — amounts, due dates, and what happens if they're missed
  • Revision limits — how many rounds are included before extra charges apply
  • Kill fee — what you're owed if the client cancels mid-project
  • IP transfer — when (and only when) ownership passes to the client
  • Dispute resolution — how conflicts get handled before anyone calls a lawyer

Most "free" templates cover only the first two. Here's how the field shakes out.

The Clauses Most Free Templates Skip

Here's what separates a contract that looks professional from one that actually protects you:

The Kill Fee

If a client cancels a project after you've started, you've already spent time on it. A kill fee clause (usually 25–50% of the remaining contract value) ensures you're compensated. Most generic templates don't include one.

Scope Lock with Change Order Language

Clients add things. "While you're at it, can you just..." is how scope creep starts. A proper contract defines the deliverables precisely and states that anything outside that scope requires a new written agreement. Without this, you're working for free every time a client asks for "one small thing."

Payment Before IP Transfer

Your work is leverage. Don't hand it over until you've been paid. A good contract specifies that intellectual property transfers to the client only upon receipt of full payment. This one clause has saved freelancers thousands.

Late Payment Fees

Net-30 is a polite fiction. Clients who can pay in 30 days often don't — unless there's a financial consequence. A clause charging 1.5–2% per month on overdue invoices changes the incentive structure immediately.

How to Pick the Right Template

If your projects are under $500 and you're working with people you trust, a basic free template might be enough. If you're doing work over $500, working with new clients, or have ever been burned before — use something built for protection, not just paperwork.

The cost of a better contract is trivial compared to a single unpaid invoice. A client who owes you $2,000 and decides to disappear isn't going to be stopped by a politely worded Google Doc.
