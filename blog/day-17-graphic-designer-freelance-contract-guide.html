The Graphic Designer's Contract Guide: Usage Rights, File Formats, and Protecting Your Work

By ContractShield · 5 min read · June 12, 2026
← Back to Blog

Design contracts have a layer of complexity that other freelance contracts don't: usage rights. You're not just selling work — you're licensing how that work can be used, by whom, on which media, for how long.

Get this wrong and you end up in situations like:

  • A logo you designed for a local business showing up on national broadcast without additional licensing
  • Print files being used for merchandise without your permission
  • A client "updating" your Figma source file and creating derivative works you don't control

Usage Rights Definition

Is this license exclusive (only this client can use it) or non-exclusive? Is it limited by media type? Limited by geography? Limited by duration?

For most brand work, a full buyout (unlimited, perpetual, worldwide) is the norm — and that's fine, but price accordingly. A logo for a bootstrapped startup is priced differently than one for a company planning to use it on product packaging in 50 countries.

File Formats Included

What files are you delivering? Which are "final" files (PDFs, PNGs, JPGs, SVGs) and which are source files (Figma, Adobe Illustrator, Photoshop)? Many designers charge separately for source files — or don't include them at all. Your contract should be explicit.

Portfolio Rights

You should always retain the right to display the work in your portfolio and on your website. Include this even if the client has full ownership.

Approval and Acceptance

A clear acceptance process prevents "we changed our mind" disputes. Define: client has 5 business days after delivery to raise objections; silence constitutes acceptance.
