How to Ask for a 50% Deposit Without Losing the Client

By ContractShield · 4 min read · June 08, 2026
← Back to Blog

The fear: "If I ask for money upfront, they'll go find a cheaper freelancer who doesn't."

The reality: clients who balk at deposits are exactly the clients who ghost invoices later.

How to Frame the Ask

Frame it as your standard process, not a specific request to this client.

Wrong: "Can I get a deposit? I've been burned before."

Right: "My standard process is 50% upfront and 50% on delivery. I've found it works well for both sides because it keeps the project moving without delays."

The "both sides" framing matters. You're not asking for protection — you're presenting a workflow that benefits the client too. Clients who've paid a deposit have a vested interest in the project moving forward efficiently.

What to Do If They Push Back

"Can we do 25% upfront?" — Yes, if the total is small. On a $500 project, $125 is symbolic. On a $5,000 project, $1,250 is real protection.

"We don't pay anything until we see work." — This is a red flag. Respond: "I completely understand. Unfortunately, my process requires a deposit before I can allocate time on my calendar and get started. If that doesn't work for your process, I'm happy to recommend other freelancers."

Put It in the Contract First

By the time you're discussing deposits verbally, you should already have sent a contract with the deposit terms written clearly. When the client sees "50% due on signing, 50% due on delivery," it's a fact on a document — not a negotiating position you're defending in real-time.

The freelancers who never struggle with deposits are the ones who made deposits non-negotiable years ago.
