Client Won't Pay Your Invoice? Here's What to Do.

← Back to Blog

The invoice is past due. You've sent one reminder. Radio silence. Now what?

Getting stiffed by a client is one of the most demoralizing things that happens in freelancing — and it happens to almost everyone eventually. This guide gives you a clear, step-by-step path from that first awkward reminder to the last resort. Follow it in order.

Before you start: Your leverage depends entirely on what's in writing. If you have a signed contract with payment terms, you're in a strong position. If you don't, the steps below still apply — but your leverage is weaker. Either way, keep reading.

The 6-Step Response to a Non-Paying Client

1

Send a Polite Reminder — Assume Good Faith First

The invoice might have gone to spam. The client might be on vacation. Their accounting system might have a bug. Most late payments at the 1–7 day mark aren't malicious — they're administrative.

Send a short, friendly email that assumes a simple oversight. This preserves the relationship and often resolves it instantly.

Email template — Day 3-7 after due date
Subject: Invoice #[NUMBER] — Quick Follow-Up Hi [Name], Just a quick follow-up on Invoice #[NUMBER] for $[AMOUNT], which was due on [DATE]. If it's slipped through the cracks, here's the invoice again: [LINK] Let me know if you have any questions. Best, [Your Name]
2

Follow Up More Directly — No More Assuming

If a week passes with no response to your first reminder, the tone shifts. This is no longer an administrative oversight. Keep it professional, but make clear that the invoice is overdue and you expect payment by a specific date.

Include the invoice again. State a clear deadline. Don't threaten yet — just be direct.

Email template — Day 10-14 after due date
Subject: Invoice #[NUMBER] — Now 10 Days Overdue Hi [Name], I haven't heard back on Invoice #[NUMBER] for $[AMOUNT], due on [DATE]. This invoice is now [X] days overdue. Please confirm receipt of this email and arrange payment by [SPECIFIC DATE — 5 business days from now]. Invoice is attached. Payment link: [LINK] Thank you, [Your Name]
3

Send a Formal Demand Letter

If no response after your second follow-up, it's time to treat this as a formal dispute. A demand letter signals that you're serious and creates a documented paper trail.

State the amount owed, cite your contract (if you have one), mention that late payment fees are accruing (if your contract includes them), and give a final deadline — typically 7-10 business days. State clearly what happens next if they don't pay: small claims court or collections.

Send it via email AND certified mail if possible. You want proof of delivery.

4

Pause or Withhold Deliverables

If you have unpaid invoices and you're still actively working for the client — stop. Politely but clearly: work pauses until the outstanding balance is cleared.

If you've already delivered all work and you had a contract with an IP transfer clause, the client doesn't legally own the work until they've paid. This is significant leverage. Make sure they understand this.

Do not threaten to publish their files or do anything with their work — just state that ownership hasn't transferred and you're open to resolving the payment issue.

5

Try Small Claims Court (It's Easier Than You Think)

Small claims court is designed for disputes exactly like this. No lawyer needed. In most US states, you can file online for $30–$75 for claims up to $5,000–$10,000 (limits vary by state). In the UK, the Money Claim Online service costs £35–£410 depending on the amount.

Here's what you need: a copy of your contract or agreement, copies of all invoices, proof of delivery (emails showing completed work), and copies of your payment reminders. That's it.

Many clients pay in full the moment they receive a court summons. The filing fee alone often resolves the dispute.

6

Use a Collections Agency or Sell the Debt

For amounts over $1,000 where court feels like too much overhead, a collections agency takes a cut (typically 25–50%) but handles everything. Agencies like Atradius, Euler Hermes, or even simpler services like Collectly work with freelancers.

Alternatively, some freelancers use invoice factoring — selling the debt at a discount to a third party who then pursues payment. You get immediate cash, they take the risk.

What About Leaving a Review?

Before you leave a bad review on Google, LinkedIn, or a freelancing platform — wait until the payment issue is formally resolved one way or another. A negative review left mid-dispute can be used against you legally in some jurisdictions, and some platforms will remove it anyway if the client disputes it.

After the dispute is resolved, an honest, factual review is fair. Stick to facts: "Did not pay Invoice #X for $Y despite two written reminders and a formal demand letter." No emotional language. Facts only.

How to Avoid This Situation Next Time

The best recovery from a non-paying client is making sure it doesn't happen again. Three things change the outcome dramatically:

  • Require a deposit. 25–50% upfront before you start any work. Non-negotiable.
  • Use a contract with late payment fees. Clients who know there's a 1.5%/month penalty on late invoices pay faster.
  • Don't transfer IP until you're paid. Your deliverables are leverage — use them.

None of this requires a lawyer. A well-written freelance contract handles all of it.
